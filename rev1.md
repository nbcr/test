Phase-Field Origin Cosmology:
A Unified Explanation for Dark Matter, Dark Energy,
and the Gravitational Constant
Shawn Amyotte
March 2026  |  Version 2.0


Abstract
We propose a cosmological framework in which our universe is one of many discrete pockets that formed within a pre-existing origin field — a structureless medium that precedes spacetime and whose sole intrinsic property is what becomes gravity inside each pocket. Universes are not embedded in a geometric bulk; rather, they form as pockets within the Higgs field, which permeates the origin field and serves as the boundary layer between the origin field and each pocket's internal spacetime. Because gravity is a property of the origin field itself — not an emergent force internal to any pocket — it communicates across phase boundaries between neighboring universes. The other fundamental forces (electromagnetic, weak, strong) crystallize only inside pockets as they cool, and cannot cross phase boundaries. This architecture provides a unified mechanism for three observed phenomena: (1) dark energy, as the continuous expansion of spacetime driven by the origin field feeding each pocket; (2) dark matter, as gravity from ordinary matter in phase-neighboring universes bleeding through the Higgs field boundary layer; and (3) the gravitational constant, as a value set externally by the origin field geometry rather than by internal pocket physics. The model makes several falsifiable predictions, including that dark matter halos trace neighboring universe mass distributions, that the CMB contains faint structural imprints of neighboring universes, and that the cosmic web filament spacing encodes the number of phase neighbors. Key open questions — particularly the mechanism of pocket formation and the precise nature of the origin field — are identified and honestly stated.



1. Introduction
Standard cosmology — the Lambda-CDM model — is remarkably successful at describing the large-scale structure of the universe. Yet it rests on two unexplained pillars: dark matter and dark energy, which together constitute approximately 95% of the universe's energy content. Both are named for what they do, not what they are. No dark matter particle has been detected despite decades of searching. The cosmological constant that drives dark energy has a value that no theory successfully derives from first principles. The gravitational constant similarly has a precise, measured value that no deeper theory predicts.
We propose that these three mysteries share a common origin, and that their explanation requires reconsidering what gravity is and where it comes from. The central claim of this paper is that gravity is not an emergent force internal to our universe — it is a property of the pre-existing medium within which our universe formed. This distinction, subtle at first, has consequences that naturally account for dark matter, dark energy, and the gravitational constant without introducing new particles or extra dimensions.
The structure of this paper is as follows. Section 2 describes the origin field and the formation of universe-pockets within it. Section 3 explains why gravity crosses phase boundaries while other forces do not. Section 4 derives the dark matter and dark energy mechanisms. Section 5 addresses the gravitational constant. Section 6 discusses the cosmic web prediction. Section 7 states testable predictions. Section 8 honestly identifies open questions. Section 9 concludes.

2. The Origin Field and Pocket Formation
2.1 The Origin Field
We posit a pre-existing medium we call the origin field. This field is not embedded in any geometric space — it precedes spacetime. It has no internal structure in the conventional sense. Time, as a concept, does not apply to it; time only exists inside pockets. The question of what existed before the origin field, or what caused it, is therefore malformed — 'before' requires time, which only exists inside pockets. This is analogous to Hawking's observation that asking what came before the Big Bang is meaningless because time itself began there, but extended here to a more general principle: time is always and only a pocket phenomenon.
The origin field has one intrinsic property: it is the substrate from which what we call gravity emerges. Inside pockets, this property manifests as the curvature of spacetime that Einstein described. Outside pockets — in the origin field itself — it exists in a more primitive form that does not require spacetime geometry to propagate. We do not yet have a mathematical description of this primitive gravity-like property, and we identify this as the primary open question of the framework.
2.2 The Higgs Field as Boundary Layer
The Higgs field is not an internal pocket phenomenon. We propose that the Higgs field permeates the origin field itself, and that pockets form within it. This is a crucial departure from standard physics, where the Higgs field is treated as one of many quantum fields that exist inside the universe.
The evidence motivating this departure is threefold. First, the Higgs field has a positive, non-zero value at every point in space — unlike most quantum fields, which have zero expectation value in their ground state. This suggests the Higgs field is not a fluctuation above some baseline, but is itself the baseline. Second, the Higgs field gives particles mass, and mass is what couples matter to the gravitational property of the origin field. This suggests the Higgs is the interface between the origin field and internal pocket physics. Third, the hierarchy problem — the fact that the Higgs field's value is 17 orders of magnitude smaller than naive calculation predicts — has no solution in standard physics. If the Higgs value is set by the origin field externally rather than by pocket physics internally, this fine-tuning is no longer mysterious: it is simply a boundary condition imposed from outside the pocket.
In this picture, mass is not merely a property of particles. It is the degree to which a particle couples to the origin field through the Higgs boundary layer. Gravity propagates through the Higgs field across phase boundaries between neighboring pockets because the Higgs field is the shared medium they all sit within.
2.3 Pocket Formation and the Origin Event
We propose that the origin field underwent a violent eruption event — the origin event — which created pockets of expanding spacetime. Each pocket is a universe. The origin event was not a singularity in the conventional sense; it was an event in the origin field that created the conditions for pocket formation through extreme energy and turbulence.
Crucially, the origin event may be ongoing. New pockets may still be forming within the origin field. This is not a Many Worlds branching process — we are not describing probability paths in a quantum wavefunction. These are physical pockets with real matter and real spacetime, forming within the same origin field.
Each pocket inherited a rotational component from the expulsion event at slightly different angles. This explains two otherwise separate observations: why galaxies spin at all, and why they spin in different directions with no single preferred axis across the universe.
The mechanism of pocket formation — what triggers a pocket, what determines its initial size and energy, and what determines when formation occurs — is an open question explicitly acknowledged in Section 8.
2.4 Phase Differences Between Universes
Pockets that formed at slightly different times, or at slightly different locations within the origin field, expanded at slightly different rates. This difference in expansion rate is what we call the phase difference between universes. Phase difference is therefore not an additional parameter added by hand — it emerges naturally from the turbulence and temperature variations of the origin event, exactly as density variations in our early universe seeded galaxy formation.
Universes with similar expansion rates have smaller phase differences. Universes with very different expansion rates have large phase differences. Phase difference is the key variable controlling inter-universe gravitational interaction: closer phases interact more strongly, larger phase differences suppress interaction.

3. Why Gravity Crosses Phase Boundaries
The standard model contains four fundamental forces: gravity, electromagnetism, the weak nuclear force, and the strong nuclear force. In our framework, these are not equivalent in origin. Three of them — electromagnetism, the weak force, and the strong force — crystallized inside pockets as they cooled from the extreme temperatures of the origin event. This is consistent with what standard physics already describes as symmetry breaking: at extreme temperatures, forces were unified; as the universe cooled, they separated into distinct interactions.
Gravity did not crystallize inside the pocket because it was never inside the pocket. It is a property of the origin field that pockets formed within. This provides a conceptual resolution to one of the deepest puzzles in physics: why gravity has resisted unification with the other three forces in every theoretical attempt. The answer, in this framework, is structural — gravity is categorically different from the other forces. It does not resist unification because of some technical obstacle; it resists unification because it belongs to a different level of reality.
Light, electromagnetism, and the strong and weak forces cannot cross phase boundaries because they are purely internal to the pocket spacetime in which they crystallized. They have no representation in the origin field and no mechanism to propagate through it. Gravity, by contrast, propagates through the Higgs field — the shared medium that all pockets sit within — and therefore communicates between neighboring universes.
The strength of this communication is controlled by two variables: phase difference and mass. Phase difference sets the channel width — how open the connection between two universes is. Mass sets the volume flowing through that channel — how much gravitational influence actually transfers. These two variables together determine how much dark matter a given region of our universe experiences from its neighbors.

4. Dark Matter and Dark Energy
4.1 Dark Matter as Neighbor Gravity
Neighboring universes contain ordinary matter — stars, gas, galaxies — governed by the same physics as our own, because all pockets share the same origin field and the same Higgs field sets the same laws everywhere. Their matter distribution is similar to ours because all pockets emerged from the same turbulent origin event with the same initial conditions, modulated by small differences in expansion rate.
The gravity of that neighboring matter bleeds through the Higgs boundary layer into our universe. We experience it as an additional gravitational pull that traces our own matter distribution but has no visible source. This is dark matter.
The reason dark matter halos trace galaxy distributions so precisely is not coincidence. Because all universes emerged from the same origin event, their large-scale structure is correlated. Dense regions in our universe correspond to dense regions in neighboring universes. The gravity bleeding through is therefore stronger where we already have more matter — amplifying existing structure rather than adding uniform background pressure. This exponential amplification explains why dark matter effects are most pronounced in galaxies and galaxy clusters, and why the dark matter distribution mirrors the visible matter distribution so closely.
Furthermore, because the origin event imprinted correlated turbulence across all pockets, the eddies and currents that seeded galaxy formation in our universe have counterparts in neighboring universes. The mirrored gravitational influence reinforces structure at the same locations across phase boundaries, producing the observed tight correlation between dark matter and baryonic matter distributions.
4.2 Dark Energy as Origin Field Expansion
Spacetime inside each pocket is not a fixed container. It is continuously being fed and expanded by the origin field. Dark energy — the accelerating expansion of the universe — is the ongoing process of the origin field generating new spacetime within each pocket. The expansion does not require fuel from within the pocket; it is driven from outside.
This picture naturally explains why the expansion is accelerating. As the pocket grows, the origin field continues to feed it at a rate that, rather than diminishing, maintains or increases the expansion velocity. The analogy is not a gas expanding in a fixed room — it is a room whose walls are being continuously built outward by an external process.
If new pockets are still forming in the origin field, the total number of phase neighbors may increase over cosmic time. This would mean the dark matter contribution to our universe increases slowly as new neighbors are born — a prediction that is in principle testable with sufficiently precise measurements of dark matter density at different cosmic epochs.

5. The Gravitational Constant
The gravitational constant G has a precise measured value of approximately 6.674 x 10^-11 N m^2 kg^-2. No theory in standard physics derives this value from deeper principles. It is simply measured and accepted as a fundamental constant.
In our framework, G is not a fundamental constant of nature in the conventional sense. It is set externally by the origin field — specifically, by the number of phase-neighboring universes and their geometric arrangement around our pocket. The strength of gravity we measure inside our universe reflects both the intrinsic gravitational property of the origin field and the specific neighborhood our pocket finds itself in.
This has a concrete implication: universes with different numbers of neighbors, or in different geometric arrangements within the origin field, would have different effective gravitational constants. G is universal within our universe precisely because all of our universe sits within the same origin field neighborhood. But it is not universal across all universes.
A corollary prediction: G should be genuinely constant across our universe to extremely high precision — not because it is a fundamental constant of nature but because the neighborhood geometry does not change on scales smaller than the inter-pocket separation. Observations suggesting variation in G across cosmic scales would require reexamination of this prediction.

6. The Cosmic Web and Neighbor Count
The cosmic web — the large-scale filamentary structure of the universe, with galaxy clusters at nodes, filaments connecting them, and vast voids between — is one of the most striking features of the observable universe. Its origin in standard cosmology is attributed to primordial density fluctuations amplified by gravity over cosmic time.
Our framework offers an additional layer of explanation. The filaments of the cosmic web mark the locations where neighboring universe gravity is reinforcing our own matter distribution. The geometry of the filament network — the characteristic spacing between filaments, the typical scale of voids — encodes the number and arrangement of phase-neighboring universes.
Specifically, we predict that the characteristic spacing of cosmic web strands is directly related to the number of phase neighbors. If the filament spacing can be measured precisely and converted to a neighbor count using the gravitational coupling mechanism described in Section 3, this provides an independent measurement of the phase neighborhood geometry — and potentially a direct measurement of the gravitational constant's external origin.
This is a concrete and falsifiable prediction. The cosmic web spacing is already measured with considerable precision by galaxy surveys. What is needed is a theoretical derivation of the relationship between neighbor count, phase difference distribution, and filament spacing — work we identify as a priority for follow-up.

7. Testable Predictions
We summarize the model's falsifiable predictions:

1.  Dark matter halos mirror visible matter distributions because neighboring universe matter is correlated with ours through shared origin event turbulence. This is already observed and the model accounts for it mechanistically.
2.  The CMB contains faint structural imprints of neighboring universes. The axis of evil — the anomalous alignment of low-multipole CMB moments — may be the gravitational shadow of a neighboring universe's mass distribution on our earliest observable light. This is a specific, testable claim against existing Planck data.
3.  Dark matter density increases over cosmic time if new pockets are still forming in the origin field, increasing the number of phase neighbors. Measurements of dark matter density at different cosmic epochs provide a test of this prediction.
4.  The cosmic web filament spacing encodes neighbor count. Once the theoretical relationship between filament spacing and phase neighbor geometry is derived, this provides an independent measurement of the model's key parameter.
5.  The Higgs field exhibits anomalous couplings at high energies reflecting its role as a boundary layer between the origin field and pocket spacetime, rather than a purely internal field. This is testable at future collider experiments.
6.  G is constant across our universe to extremely high precision because it reflects the origin field neighborhood geometry, which does not vary within a single pocket.
7.  Galaxy rotation varies with no single preferred spin axis across the universe, because each pocket inherited rotation from the origin event at slightly different angles. This is already observed and the model accounts for it.

8. Open Questions
We explicitly acknowledge the following unresolved problems, which we consider priorities for future theoretical work:

1.  Pocket formation mechanism. What triggers a pocket to form within the origin field? What determines its initial energy, size, and expansion rate? We have no mechanism for this. We know the consequences of pocket formation but not its cause.
2.  Pre-spacetime gravity. Gravity as we understand it is the curvature of spacetime. If spacetime does not exist in the origin field, what is the primitive property of the origin field that becomes gravity inside pockets? This is the deepest open question in the framework and likely requires new mathematical tools.
3.  Quantitative coupling derivation. The two-variable mechanism (phase difference sets channel width, mass sets volume) needs to be derived mathematically from the Higgs boundary layer physics. Without this, the dark matter amount can be explained after the fact but not predicted.
4.  Nature of the origin field. We describe the origin field by its effects — it generates pockets, it permeates the Higgs field, it has a gravity-like property. We do not describe what it is made of, if that question is even meaningful. It may be what physicists call the quantum vacuum — not nothing, but a structureless medium with properties — but this identification is speculative.
5.  Reproducing general relativistic predictions. Einstein's description of gravity as spacetime curvature has been tested to extraordinary precision — gravitational waves, light bending, GPS corrections. Any mechanism that proposes gravity has a deeper origin must reproduce these results exactly inside pockets. We have not demonstrated this.

9. Relation to Existing Work
The idea that dark matter could be ordinary matter in neighboring branes or universes has been proposed previously, notably by Liu (2003) and in informal preprints exploring adjacent-universe gravity. Our framework differs in several important respects: we do not use a geometric bulk or extra dimensions; we do not rely on brane-world junction conditions; and we propose a specific physical mechanism — the Higgs field as boundary layer — for how gravity crosses phase boundaries while other forces do not.
The concept of emergent spacetime — that spacetime is not fundamental but arises from something more basic — is actively pursued in quantum gravity research, including entropic gravity and loop quantum cosmology. Our framework is consistent with the broad conclusion that spacetime is emergent, but differs in locating the source of emergence in the origin field rather than in quantum entanglement or entropy within the universe.
The black hole cosmology hypothesis, developed formally by Lee Smolin, proposes that universes are born from black holes in parent universes. We consider this a separate and potentially compatible idea, but do not incorporate it into the present framework. We note that if black hole interiors create new pockets, those pockets would be causally isolated — not even gravity would communicate across the event horizon, by the author's own reasoning — and therefore would not contribute to dark matter through the phase-neighbor mechanism.

10. Conclusion
We have presented a cosmological framework built on a single structural claim: gravity is a property of the pre-existing origin field within which universes form as pockets, not an emergent force internal to any universe. The Higgs field permeates this origin field and serves as the boundary layer between it and each pocket's internal spacetime. This architecture provides a unified account of dark matter (gravity from phase-neighboring universes), dark energy (continuous spacetime generation by the origin field), and the gravitational constant (set externally by neighborhood geometry rather than internal pocket physics).
The framework makes falsifiable predictions against existing and future data, explicitly acknowledges its open questions, and does not introduce new particles, extra dimensions, or geometric bulk. It offers a conceptual resolution to why gravity has resisted unification with the other three forces — not because of a technical obstacle, but because gravity belongs to a categorically different level of reality.
The primary open questions — the mechanism of pocket formation, the mathematical description of pre-spacetime gravity, and the quantitative derivation of the Higgs coupling — define a research program for theoretical physicists with the appropriate tools. We present this framework as a conceptual foundation for that program.
